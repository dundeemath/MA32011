7  Bifurcations in 2D systems

7.1 Introduction

Previously, we described bifurcations in 1D systems. In this chapter we generalise these ideas to account for the more complex behaviour of 2D systems.

We will find that the previous notion of a bifurcation involving the creation or annihilation of fixed points or a change in their stability still holds. However, we must generalise these ideas to account for the presence of limit cycles in 2D systems.

Consider a system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}=f(x,y;\mu), \\ \dot{y}=g(x,y;\mu), \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu\) is a bifurcation parameter and \(f\) and \(g\) are continuously differentiable functions.

Definition 1
A bifurcation occurs at \(\mu =\mu_c\) if the phase portraits for \(\mu < \mu_c\) and \(\mu > \mu_c\) are not topologically equivalent.

7.2 Saddle node, transcritical and pitchfork bifurcations

The bifurcations introduced in Chapter 3 (i.e. saddle node, transcritical and pitchfork) have analogues in two dimensions. Near the bifurcation the dynamics reduce to a 1D centre manifold.

Example 7.1 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = \mu - x^2 \\ \dot{y} &= -y. \end{aligned} \]

Sketch the phase portrait for different values of \(\mu\).

Solution
FPs at \((\pm\sqrt{\mu},0)\) for \(\mu >0\).

Let \(\mu>0\).

At \((\sqrt{\mu},0)\),the eigenvalues are \(-2\sqrt{\mu}\) and \(-1\). Hence it is a stable node.

At \((-\sqrt{\mu},0)\),the eigenvalues are \(2\sqrt{\mu}\) and \(-1\). Hence it is a saddle.

At \(\mu=0\) the FPs have collided and the FP at (0,0) is half-stable.

It is a saddle-node bifurcation.

Example 7.2 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = -ax+y \\ \dot{y} &= \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}-by. \end{aligned} \] where \(a,b > 0\).

Show that there is a saddle node bifurcation at a critical value \(a=a_c\).

Solution
FPs: \((0,0)\) and \[ \left(\frac{1\pm\sqrt{1-4a^2b^2}}{2ab}, \frac{a(1\pm\sqrt{1-4a^2b^2})}{2ab}\right). \]

FPs collide when \[ 4a^2b^2=1 \implies a=:a_c=\frac{1}{2b}. \]

At the bifurcation the FP is \((1,a)\).

The Jacobian is \[ \begin{pmatrix} -a & 1 \\ \frac{2x}{(1+x^2)^2} & -b \end{pmatrix}. \]

At (0,0) the eigenvalues are \(-a\) and \(-b\). Hence it is always a stable node (so long as we are not in the degenerate case \(a\neq b\)).

Note that \(\textrm{tr} A<0\). Hence linear stability will be determined by the determinant \[ \det{A}=ab-\frac{2x^*}{(1+{x^*}^2)^2}. \]

It is messy to substitute the FPs here. Instead note the identity \[ \frac{x^*}{1+{x^*}^2}= ab. \]

Hence \[ \det{A}=ab-\frac{2ab}{1+{x^*}^2} = ab\frac{{x^*}^2-1}{(1+{x^*}^2)} \]

Hence for \(x^*>1\), \(\det{A}<0\) and the FP is a saddle.

For \(x^*<1\), \(\det{A}>0\) and the FP is linearly stable (it is a stable node).

Hence there a saddle node bifurcation at \(a=a_c\).

Example 7.3 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = \mu x + y +\sin x \\ \dot{y} &= x-y. \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu \in \Re\). Show that a supercritical pitchfork bifurcation occurs at the origin and determine the bifurcation value \(\mu_c\).

Similar examples can be considered for transcritical and pitchfork bifurcations. A common property of all the above bifurcations is that one of the eigenvalues of the Jacobian is zero. Next we consider a new mechanism through which bifurcations may arise.

7.3 Hopf bifurcations

Hopf bifurcations describe a bifurcation in which oscillatory solutions emerge. The key idea with Hopf bifurcations is that there is a pair of complex conjugate eigenvalues that cross the imaginary axis. Hence at the bifurcation point the real part of the eigenvalues is zero but the imaginary part is non-zero. Thus the Hopf bifurcation is qualitatively distinct from the previous bifurcations we have considered (characterised by at least one eigenvalue being zero at the bifurcation point).

Theorem 2
Hopf bifurcation theorem:

If:

  1. eigenvalues are complex conjugates
  2. real part crosses zero
  3. a nondegeneracy condition holds

Then a limit cycle emerges.

Hopf bifurcations can be classified as supercritical, subcritical or degenerate. An analytic criterion exists to classify but it often cumbersome to compute.

Supercritical Hopf bifurcations are characterised by small-amplitude oscillations close to the bifurcation point.

Subcritical Hopf bifurcations are characterised by large-amplitude oscillations that emerge at the bifurcation point. Thus a system can transition from a stable steady state to large oscillations.

7.3.1 Supercritical Hopf

The supercritical Hopf is sometimes known as ‘soft’ in the engineering literature: at the bifurcation point small amplitude oscillations emerge.

Example 7.4 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{r} & = \mu r - r^3\\ \dot{\theta} &= \omega+br^2. \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu\), \(\omega\) and \(b\) are constants.

Show that the origin undergoes a supercritical Hopf bifurcation at \(\mu=0\).

Rules of thumb:

  • The size of the limit cycle grows continuously from zero and increases proportionally to \(\sqrt{\mu-\mu_c}\) for \(\mu\) close to \(\mu_c\).
  • The frequency of the limit cycle is given approximately by \(\omega=\Im \lambda\) evaluated at \(\mu=\mu_c\). The period is therefore approximately \(2\pi/\Im(\lambda)\).

In more general cases the limit cycle is elliptical rather than circular and the imaginary part of the eigenvalue also depends on \(\mu\).

7.4 Global bifurcations of cycles

The Hopf bifurcation can be studies using linear stability analysis, i.e. by considering behaviour of dynamical system close to a fixed point.

However, the Hopf bifurcation is not the only way in which limit cycles arise in 2D dynamical systems. Another class of bifurcations called global bifurcations, include the following bifurcation types:

  • Saddle node bifurcation of limit cycles
  • Infinite period bifurcation
  • Homoclinic bifurcation

Each of the bifurcation types shows signature dependence of properties of the limit cycle (e.g. amplitude, period) on the magnitude of the bifurcation parameter.

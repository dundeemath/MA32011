7  Bifurcations in 2D systems

7.1 Introduction

Previously, we described bifurcations in 1D systems. In this chapter we generalise these ideas to account for the more complex behaviour of 2D systems.

We will find that the previous notion of a bifurcation involving the creation or annihilation of fixed points or a change in their stability still holds. However, we must generalise these ideas to account for the presence of limit cycles in 2D systems.

Consider a system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}=f(x,y;\mu), \\ \dot{y}=g(x,y;\mu), \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu\) is a bifurcation parameter and \(f\) and \(g\) are continuously differentiable functions.

Definition 1
A bifurcation occurs at \(\mu =\mu_c\) if the phase portraits for \(\mu < \mu_c\) and \(\mu > \mu_c\) are not topologically equivalent.

7.2 Saddle node, transcritical and pitchfork bifurcations

The bifurcations introduced in Chapter 3 (i.e. saddle node, transcritical and pitchfork) have analogues in two dimensions. Near the bifurcation the dynamics reduce to a 1D centre manifold.

Example 7.1 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = \mu - x^2 \\ \dot{y} &= -y. \end{aligned} \]

Sketch the phase portrait for different values of \(\mu\).

Solution
FPs at \((\pm\sqrt{\mu},0)\) for \(\mu >0\).

Let \(\mu>0\).

At \((\sqrt{\mu},0)\),the eigenvalues are \(-2\sqrt{\mu}\) and \(-1\). Hence it is a stable node.

At \((-\sqrt{\mu},0)\),the eigenvalues are \(2\sqrt{\mu}\) and \(-1\). Hence it is a saddle.

At \(\mu=0\) the FPs have collided and the FP at (0,0) is half-stable.

It is a saddle-node bifurcation.

Example 7.2 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = -ax+y \\ \dot{y} &= \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}-by. \end{aligned} \] where \(a,b > 0\).

Show that there is a saddle node bifurcation at a critical value \(a=a_c\).

Solution
FPs: \((0,0)\) and \[ \left(\frac{1\pm\sqrt{1-4a^2b^2}}{2ab}, \frac{a(1\pm\sqrt{1-4a^2b^2})}{2ab}\right). \]

FPs collide when \[ 4a^2b^2=1 \implies a=:a_c=\frac{1}{2b}. \]

At the bifurcation the FP is \((1,a)\).

The Jacobian is \[ \begin{pmatrix} -a & 1 \\ \frac{2x}{(1+x^2)^2} & -b \end{pmatrix}. \]

At (0,0) the eigenvalues are \(-a\) and \(-b\). Hence it is always a stable node (so long as we are not in the degenerate case \(a\neq b\)).

Note that \(\textrm{tr} A<0\). Hence linear stability will be determined by the determinant \[ \det{A}=ab-\frac{2x^*}{(1+{x^*}^2)^2}. \]

It is messy to substitute the FPs here. Instead note the identity \[ \frac{x^*}{1+{x^*}^2}= ab. \]

Hence \[ \det{A}=ab-\frac{2ab}{1+{x^*}^2} = ab\frac{{x^*}^2-1}{(1+{x^*}^2)} \]

Hence for \(x^*<1\), \(\det{A}<0\) and the FP is a saddle.

For \(x^*>1\), \(\det{A}>0\) and the FP is linearly stable (it is a stable node).

Hence there a saddle node bifurcation at \(a=a_c\).

Example 7.3 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} & = \mu x + y +\sin x \\ \dot{y} &= x-y. \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu \in \Re\). Show that a supercritical pitchfork bifurcation occurs at the origin and determine the bifurcation value \(\mu_c\).

Similar examples can be considered for transcritical and pitchfork bifurcations. A common property of all the above bifurcations is that one of the eigenvalues of the Jacobian is zero. Next we consider a new mechanism through which bifurcations may arise.

7.3 Hopf bifurcations

Hopf bifurcations describe a bifurcation in which oscillatory solutions emerge. The key idea with Hopf bifurcations is that there is a pair of complex conjugate eigenvalues that cross the imaginary axis. Hence at the bifurcation point the real part of the eigenvalues is zero but the imaginary part is non-zero. Thus the Hopf bifurcation is qualitatively distinct from the previous bifurcations we have considered (characterised by at least one eigenvalue being zero at the bifurcation point).

Theorem 2
Hopf bifurcation theorem:

Consider a system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}=f(x,y;\mu), \\ \dot{y}=g(x,y;\mu), \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu\) is a bifurcation parameter. Suppose that there exists a FP with corresponding pair of complex eigenvalues \(\lambda=\hat{\mu}+i\hat{\omega}\)

  1. eigenvalues are complex conjugates (\(\hat{\omega}\neq0\))
  2. real part crosses zero at the bifurcation (\(\mu=\mu_c\), \(\hat{\mu}=0\).)
  3. a nondegeneracy condition holds (i.e. the system behaviour is robust to small changes in other parameters/inclusion of other small terms). A formula for this condition can be stated but we will not do so here.

Then a limit cycle emerges.

Hopf bifurcations can be classified as supercritical, subcritical or degenerate. An analytic criterion exists to classify but it often cumbersome to compute.

Supercritical Hopf bifurcations are characterised by small-amplitude oscillations close to the bifurcation point.

In contrast, subcritical Hopf bifurcations are characterised by large-amplitude oscillations that emerge at the bifurcation point. Thus a system can transition from a stable steady state to large oscillations.

7.3.1 Supercritical Hopf

The supercritical Hopf is sometimes known as ‘soft’ in the engineering literature: at the bifurcation point small amplitude oscillations emerge.

Example 7.4 Consider the system \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{r} & = \mu r - r^3\\ \dot{\theta} &= \omega+br^2. \end{aligned} \] where \(\mu\), \(\omega\) and \(b\) are constants.

Show that the origin undergoes a supercritical Hopf bifurcation at \(\mu=0\).

Solution
The \(r\) equation decouples and can be treated as a 1D flow. Fixed points of the \(r\) equation are \(r^*=0\) and \(r^*_+=\sqrt{\mu}, \mu >0\).

When \(r=r^*_+\), \[ \theta=(w+b\mu)t+\theta_0, \] i.e. the equation of a circle in parametric form.

Compute \[ f'(r)=\mu-3r^2. \]

At \(r^*=0\), \(f'(0)=\mu\). Hence \(r^*=0\) is linearly stable for \(\mu<0\) and linearly unstable for \(\mu>0\).

When \(\mu>0\), \[ f'(\sqrt{\mu})=-2\mu. \] Hence \(r^*=\sqrt{\mu}\) is linearly stable.

When \(\mu=0\), there is a bifurcation.

Considering the full system, when \(\mu>0\) there is a stable limit cycle given by the circle \[ r=r^{*}=\sqrt{\mu}, \theta=(\omega +b\mu)t. \]

When \(\mu <0\) there is a stable spiral. At \(\mu=0\) there is a supercritical Hopf bifurcation; the stable spiral bifurcates to a stable limit cycle.

We can slso proceed by considering the system in Cartesian coordinates. Define \[ x=r\cos(\theta), \quad y=r\sin(\theta). \] Changing variables \[ \dot{x}=\mu x - \omega y + cubic terms, \quad \dot{y}=\omega x + \mu y + cubic terms. \]

Hence \((0,0)\) is a fixed point. The Jacobian is given by \[ A=\begin{pmatrix} \mu & - \omega \\ \omega & \mu. \end{pmatrix}. \]

The eigenvalues are \[ \lambda_{\pm}=\mu\pm i\omega. \]

Note that \(\omega \neq 0\) and the transverality condition is satisfied.

The system has a supercritical Hopf bifurcation at \(\mu=0\). It satisifes the conditions for a Hopf bifurcation. It is supercritical because a stable fixed bifurcates to stable limit cycle, with an amplitude that grows with \(\sqrt{\mu}\).

Exercises:

  • Choose \(\mu<0\) and observe that the oscillations decay (you might need to increase the time, \(T\), to see this)
  • Make \(\mu>0\). Explore the initial conditions (e.g. initialising inside the limit cycle the solution spirals out to the limit cycle).

An app demonstrates the emergence of small amplitude oscillations at a supercritical Hopf bifurcation. The black circle represents the limit cycle: \(r=\sqrt{\mu}\).

Rules of thumb:

  • The size of the limit cycle grows continuously from zero and increases proportionally to \(\sqrt{\mu-\mu_c}\) for \(\mu\) close to \(\mu_c\).
  • The frequency of the limit cycle is given approximately by \(\omega=\Im \lambda\) evaluated at \(\mu=\mu_c\). The period is therefore approximately \(2\pi/\Im(\lambda)\).

In more general cases the limit cycle is elliptical rather than circular and the imaginary part of the eigenvalue also depends on \(\mu\).

7.3.2 Subcritical Hopf

At a subcritical Hopf bifurcation, a stable fixed bifurcates such that trajectories jump to a distant attractor (e.g. a limit cycle or another fixed point).

We proceed by considering the example \[ \begin{aligned} \dot{r}&=\mu r+r^3-r^5 \\ \dot{\theta}&=\omega+br^2. \end{aligned} \]

Show that there is a supercritical Hopf bifurcation at \(\mu=\mu_c=0\).

Solution
The \(r\) equation again decouples and can therefore be treated independently. The \(r\) equation has FP \[ r^*=0 \] or \[ {r^*}^4-{r^*}^2-\mu. \] Hence \[ r^*=\pm \sqrt{ \frac{1\pm\sqrt{1+4\mu}}{2}}. \]

Requiring that \(r^*\geq 0\), the FPs can be classified as follows:

\(r^*=0\) is always a FP.

  • Case 1: \(\mu<-\frac{1}{4}\), no other FPs
  • Case 2: \(-\frac{1}{4}<\mu<0\), Two other FPs: \(r^*_{\pm}= \sqrt{ \frac{1\pm\sqrt{1+4\mu}}{2}}\)
  • Case 3: \(\mu >0\), One other FP: \(r^*_+= \sqrt{ \frac{1+\sqrt{1+4\mu}}{2}}\)

Case 1: \(r^*=0\) is linearly stable.

Case 2: \(r^*=0\) is linearly stable. \(r^*_+\) is linearly unstable. \(r^*_-\) is linearly stable.

Case 3: \(r^*=0\) is linearly unstable. \(r^*_+\) is linearly stable.

Reconsidering the full dynamical system, the identified solutions with \(r^*>0\) are limit cycles with angular frequency \[ \omega+b{r^*}^2. \]

7.4 Global bifurcations of cycles

The Hopf bifurcation can be studies using linear stability analysis, i.e. by considering behaviour of dynamical system close to a fixed point.

However, the Hopf bifurcation is not the only way in which limit cycles arise in 2D dynamical systems. Another class of bifurcations, known as global bifurcations, include the following bifurcation types:

  • Saddle node bifurcation of limit cycles
  • Infinite period bifurcation
  • Homoclinic bifurcation

Each of the bifurcation types shows signature dependence of properties of the limit cycle (e.g. amplitude, period) on the magnitude of the bifurcation parameter.

7.5 Stability of Limit Cycles in General Systems

In previous examples of Hopf bifurcations, we determined the stability of limit cycles by rewriting the system in polar coordinates where the radial dynamics were independent of the angular variable \(\theta\). This allowed stability to be determined directly from the radial equation.

This raises the natural question: how do we determine the stability of a limit cycle for a general system:

\[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= f(x,y) \\ \dot{y} &= g(x,y) ? \end{aligned} \]

7.5.1 Poincaré Maps

One technique for classifying the stability of a general limit cycle is to construct a Poincaré map (also called a first return map).

Definition 3
Poincaré section

A Poincaré section is an \((N-1)\)–dimensional hypersurface that intersects the limit cycle transversally.

In a 2D system, this hypersurface is simply a line in the phase plane.

The idea is simple:

  • Choose a transversal section, \(\Sigma\)
  • Follow trajectories starting near the limit cycle
  • Record where they next intersect \(\Sigma\)

This produces a discrete dynamical system \[ x_{n+1} = F(x_n), \]

where \(x_n\) represents the coordinate where the trajectory intersects the Poincaré section. The fixed points of this map correspond to periodic orbits (limit cycles) of the continuous system.

7.5.2 Stability of the Limit Cycle

If the limit cycle is stable:

  • Small perturbations decay
  • Trajectories return toward the fixed point of the map

If the limit cycle is unstable:

  • Small perturbations grow
  • Trajectories move away from the fixed point

The eigenvalues of the linearisation of the Poincaré map about a fixed point are called Floquet multipliers. These determine the stability of the limit cycle.

In a 2D system:

  • The Poincaré section is 1–dimensional
  • There is only one nontrivial Floquet multiplier
  • The second multiplier is always 1 (corresponding to motion along the cycle)

In most cases, the Floquet multipliers cannot be computed analytically and must be estimated numerically.
